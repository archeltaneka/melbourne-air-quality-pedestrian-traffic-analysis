Air Quality Check A Day, Keeps The Pollution Away

Air pollution is a growing concern in Australia, contributing to chronic respiratory diseases and even deaths. Personally, I've noticed how poor air quality affects my endurance and energy during outdoor activities like running. This made me wonder: How does air quality influence daily life, particularly pedestrian activity? With urban populations increasing and air pollution worsening, understanding this relationship is critical for creating healthier, more livable cities.

Project Aims

  • To identify seasonal trends in air quality, particularly harmful pollutants.
  • To explore the correlation between pollutant levels and daily pedestrian counts.
  • To provide policymakers and health professionals with actionable insights into mitigating health risks caused by poor air quality.

Pollutant Information & Health Impact

Carbon Monoxide (CO)

Colorless, odorless gas from vehicle emissions.

Health Impact: Reduces oxygen delivery to organs, causes headaches, dizziness, and can be fatal at high concentrations.

Measurement: Parts per million (ppm)

Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2)

Reddish-brown gas from vehicle exhaust and industrial processes.

Health Impact: Irritates respiratory system, aggravates asthma, reduces lung function.

Measurement: Parts per billion (ppb)

Ozone (O3)

Secondary pollutant formed from sunlight reacting with NOx and VOCs.

Health Impact: Causes chest pain, coughing, throat irritation, worsens bronchitis and emphysema.

Measurement: Parts per billion (ppb)

Particulate Matter (PM2.5 & PM10)

Tiny particles from combustion, dust, and industrial processes.

Health Impact: Penetrates deep into lungs and bloodstream, linked to heart disease, stroke, and lung cancer.

Measurement: Micrograms per cubic meter (Œºg/m¬≥)

Key Insights from the Data

Seasonal Patterns: PM2.5 and PM10 levels peak during winter months (June-August) due to increased heating and stable atmospheric conditions.
Rush Hour Impact: NO2 and CO concentrations show clear peaks during morning (7-9 AM) and evening (5-7 PM) rush hours.
Ozone Formation: O3 levels are highest during sunny afternoons when UV radiation drives photochemical reactions.
Weekend Effect: Pollutant levels are generally lower on weekends, particularly for traffic-related pollutants like CO and NO2.
